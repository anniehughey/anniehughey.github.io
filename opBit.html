Operation Bitwise

UX designers working on games have the difficult task of balancing the art of creating fun user problems while also removing them.

Operation Bitwise is a slide-until-blocked strategic puzzle game. Players control the two viruses, Vi and Russ, who must cooperatively interact with each other to 'hack' each level. Each level presents a puzzle created by the adversarial security system that players must solve to hack deeper into the computer. Each computer houses a set of levels with similar difficulty levels and gameplay objects. Once the final level of a computer is beaten, the players get promoted, and move up through the ranks of hackers at Info Boost.

  • The Challenge

    Gr8 Games, a team of six undergraduate students with varying skills and backgrounds were tasked with creating a mobile game throughout the spring semester to be presented at the Game Design Initiative at Cornell's spring showcase. The initial design challenge was that the core game mechanic must be completely unique. Following an Agile development process, our team presented the newest iteration of our game every two weeks.

  • The Approach

    Team Workflow

    Because the team comprised of six members, four programmers and two designers, from very different backgrounds, setting expectations and standards was extremely important for the success of the game. In our team workflow document we established weekly meeting times, creative conflict resolution procedures, task tables, file sharing expectations, communication methods, and interal & external deadline expectations. We then planned out tasks and goals for each sprint and prototype release including: Non-digital Prototype, Gameplay Prototype, Technical Prototype, Alpha Release, Closed Beta Release, Open Beta Release, and the GDIAC Showcase.

    Initial Design Goal

    Operation Bitwise is an ominous slide until blocked puzzle game in which the player takes on the role of a hacker. As the player infiltrates deeper into each computer system, the challenges posed by security system grow increasingly difficult. OpBit introduces an interesting cooperation mechanic where the player controls Vi and Russ who must work together to reach their respective goals.

    Emotion: Perplexingly Simple, Mysterious, Intense

    Target Audience: Strategic puzzle fans.

  • Core Mechanic

    Strategic gameplay that involves premeditated action of both Vi and Russ. Using unanchored blocks, Operation Bitwise creates dynamic puzzles which stimulate players to invent unique and creative solutions.

    Story

    You work for Info Boost as a technician, selling “antivirus software.” In truth, you infect computers and break through their security to retrieve sensitive data. With the help of Vi and Russ, any previously impregnable system is rendered helplessly vulnerable. Take the controls, and you can hack any security system.

    Market Analysis

    Slayaway Camp: In Slayaway Camp, the player moves to the end of the path. When the player hits (and kills) a camper, the camper’s death causes some elements of the playfield to change. Operation Bitwise depends on more physics-based collisions to move the blocks in comparison to Slayaway Camp’s methods.

    Pokemon Ice Puzzles: The Pokemon Ice Puzzles have almost exclusively “anchored” blocks and allow the player to think much further ahead, as the path is immutable. While most are anchored, some of the blocks in Operation Bitwise will be unanchored and will allow the player to solve the puzzles in a slightly different manner.

    Catrap: Catrap does not have the the sliding block design that Operation Bitwise does, but it does have some levels that require the player to switch between controlling Catgirl and Catboy during the same level in order to assist one another to complete the puzzle. Very similarly, Operation Bitwise requires the player to collaboratively move Vi and Russ.

    Observations & Insights

    After evaluating our competitors, we decided to focus on two unique characteristics of our game.

    Character Teamwork: Because the player controls two characters, each with a different target tile, we are able to design interesting puzzles that force the player to use both characters in concert. The player must devise a strategy that involves both characters’ advantages and disadvantages, which stem from their positioning among the game tiles, in order to pass each level.

    Creative Thinking: To solve puzzles, the player must use Vi and Russ to move the unanchored tiles to reshape the environment to the player’s advantage. This dynamic nature of Operation Bitwise stimulates the player to invent unique and creative solutions to each puzzle.
